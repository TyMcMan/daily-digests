Agents-A1: Scaling the Horizon, Not the Parameters — Reaching Trillion-Parameter Performance with a 35B Agent

Introduces Agents-A1, a 35B Mixture-of-Experts agentic model that matches trillion-parameter-scale systems by scaling the agent horizon rather than raw parameter count. A three-stage pipeline — full-domain SFT, domain-specific teacher models, and multi-teacher domain-routed on-policy distillation with salient vocabulary alignment — produces trajectories averaging 45K tokens. Weights released on Hugging Face.

Why it matters: Achieves SOTA on SEAL-0 (56.4), FrontierScience-Olympiad (79.0), and IFEval (94.8), competing with GPT-5.5 and Kimi-K2.6 at a fraction of the compute — a credible alternative to expensive parameter scaling for agent builders.

AsyncOPD: How Stale Can On-Policy Distillation Be?

Studies the staleness problem in asynchronous on-policy distillation (OPD) for LLM post-training. Shows forward KL is robust to stale rollouts while reverse KL is vulnerable; recomputing the advantage at learner time is the best corrective. The resulting AsyncOPD system achieves 1.6–3.8× throughput improvement over synchronous training with matched accuracy.

Why it matters: A principled drop-in approach to decouple rollout generation from learner updates in fine-tuning pipelines — a direct throughput win for teams training reasoning models at scale.

'It's Hard to Eval' Is a Product Smell — Hamel Husain

Husain argues that the common objection "our product is hard to eval" is itself a signal of product design problems: artifacts that are hard for engineers to verify are typically hard for users to verify too, and users must redo the work from scratch to check correctness. The post reframes evaluability as a core product quality requirement, not a measurement afterthought.

Why it matters: Forces AI product teams to define verifiability at the design stage — if you can't write a quick eval for your output, you probably have an unclear product spec. A sharp mindset shift that prevents eval-theatre.

Context vs Prompt vs Loop vs Harness Engineering — ExplainX.ai

Clarifies four distinct engineering disciplines practitioners frequently conflate: prompt engineering (single-turn instruction design), context engineering (managing the context window), loop engineering (designing the agent's action-observation cycle), and harness engineering (infrastructure for trustworthy autonomous systems). Each has different failure modes, tools, and ownership boundaries.

Why it matters: Gives teams a clean vocabulary to split responsibilities across roles and debug failures at the right layer — knowing whether a problem is a context problem vs. a loop problem vs. a harness problem changes the fix entirely.
