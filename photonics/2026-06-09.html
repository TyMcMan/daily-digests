📄 Research & Labs

QuiX Quantum Installs Feed-Forward Control Unit for Universal Photonic QC

QuiX Quantum has installed its Feed-Forward Control Unit (FFCU), enabling real-time adaptive operations within its photonic quantum computing stack at a deterministic 150 ns latency. The FFCU combines a dual-FPGA processing bus with a 32×32 input/output routing matrix, converting single-photon detector signals into phase corrections before the target photon can travel 30 m. This closes the measurement-based feedback loop required for universal MBQC and bridges QuiX's photonic stack to classical HPC networks. Why it matters: Feed-forward is the hard real-time control problem that has held back photonic QC universality — this is a concrete demonstration of the solution stack at the component level.

Heriot-Watt FreeForm Photonics Spin-Out Targets 50%+ Reduction in Assembly Cost

FreeForm Photonics, a Heriot-Watt University spin-out backed by Scottish Enterprise's High Growth Spinout Programme, is commercialising a laser-based process that writes passive alignment features directly into optical glass components during manufacturing, eliminating post-production manual calibration — currently >50% of photonics production cost. Tolerances reach sub-micron, matching active-alignment precision. Target application domains: fibre-optic sensing, quantum computing systems, and optical communications infrastructure. Why it matters: Assembly cost is the primary bottleneck to affordable photonic packaging at volume; a laser-direct passive-alignment process could transform the economics of PIC module manufacturing.

🇪🇺 EU Photonics & Manufacturing

EU Chips Act 2.0 and the Integrated Photonics Industry

The European Commission adopted the Chips Act 2.0 proposal on June 3, 2026. For the photonics industry, the key change is explicit inclusion of photonic integrated circuit technologies as a named strategic pillar within the Chips for Europe Initiative 2.0, alongside six funding channels: PIXEurope (€380M), APECS advanced packaging (€726M), and Quantum pilots including P4Q (€52M). The analysis notes approved projects including Ephos IT (€40M, glass-based PICs) and CamGraPhIC (€211M, optical transceivers). Why it matters: Formal statutory status in the Chips Act anchors multi-year R&D and pilot-line funding for EU PIC manufacturing — directly supporting the supply chain that EU photonic compute startups (including in the Akhetonics ecosystem) depend on.

European Photonics Firms Target Advance in Quantum Networking

A coalition of European photonics companies is pursuing industrialisation of quantum networking components under the P4Q (Photonics for Quantum) pilot, a 29-partner consortium supported by €25M EU funding (matched nationally across 12 countries). The programme targets TRL-8/MRL-8 readiness across multiple photonic quantum platforms including linear optical, silicon-photonic, and InP-based components for QKD and quantum repeaters. Why it matters: Quantum networking drives demand for single-photon sources, photon-number-resolving detectors, and low-loss PIC platforms — the same component ecosystem underpinning photonic compute.
