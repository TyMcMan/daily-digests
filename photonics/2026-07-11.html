Measurement-Based Quantum Computing on a Photonic Chip

Huster, Hohmann, Edelmann, and Barz (Stuttgart) demonstrate MBQC on an integrated silicon photonic chip generating photonic graph states with up to four qubits. They achieve fidelities of 83.5 ± 1.8% for four-photon star graph states and 75.6 ± 1.1% for linear graph states. The chip implements MBQC-based single- and two-qubit gates and runs Grover's search and Deutsch–Jozsa on-chip via adaptive measurements. Why it matters: MBQC on silicon photonics avoids the need for deterministic photon-photon interactions — this result shows the platform is viable for computation, not just state generation, with commercially compatible processes.

Physics-Informed Data-Driven Modeling for Optical Communications and Computing

Teofilovic, Zibar, Da Ros et al. (DTU) compare three modeling paradigms — white-box (physics), black-box (data-driven), and hybrid physics-informed — across benchmark use cases including optical amplifiers, directly modulated lasers, and interferometer meshes used in photonic neural networks. Physics-informed models consistently outperform pure data-driven approaches on data efficiency and generalizability, while remaining faster than full physics simulation. Why it matters: Interferometer mesh digital twins are foundational for inverse-designing optical neural networks and MZI-based compute PICs — knowing which modeling approach to use at which scale is directly applicable to PIC EDA tooling.

Laser Fingerprints Provide Rapid Device Authentication

KAUST researchers have demonstrated that the unique optical fingerprints of semiconductor lasers — arising from manufacturing-process variations in cavity geometry, gain medium, and mirror reflectivity — can be used as unclonable device identifiers. Combined with an AI classifier, the system authenticates devices in milliseconds without cryptographic keys or shared secrets. The technique works on standard laser diodes with no hardware modification. Why it matters: PUF (physically unclonable function) authentication using laser signatures is directly applicable to secure PIC-based sensing and communication modules — a potential new design primitive for photonic security ICs.
