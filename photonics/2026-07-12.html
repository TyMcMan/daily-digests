Penrose-Wave Amplification Demonstrated in Lab-Scale Resonator; Photonic Chip Integration Targeted Next

CUNY ASRC researchers built a ring-shaped network of electronic resonators that simulates ultrafast rotation without physical movement, demonstrating Penrose–Zel'dovich wave amplification — an effect from black hole physics — in a table-top electromagnetic system. Waves with the correct rotational signature extracted energy from the synthetic rotation field and were amplified. The team explicitly identifies scaling to photonic and quantum-optical frequencies as the next step, citing implications for novel photonic resonator designs, quantum optical signal processing, and next-generation photonic chip architectures. Why it matters: A new physical amplification mechanism validated in hardware, with the research group already pointing at photonic chip integration — relevant for unconventional resonator and oscillator design in PICs.

KAUST: AI-Authenticated Laser-Chaos PICs for Hardware Security at AI Infrastructure Scale

A KAUST Integrated Photonics Lab team published in Nature Electronics a system using laser chaos — optoelectronic physical unclonable functions (PUFs) — to generate unique per-device fingerprints, authenticated in real time by an AI model with ultra-low latency and minimal energy consumption. The paper, "Chaos-based scalable optoelectronic physical unclonable functions with AI-driven dynamic authentication," targets large-scale IoT, cloud, and AI infrastructure. Why it matters: Laser-based PICs as hardware roots-of-trust for AI data-center fabrics — a convergence of photonic integration and hardware security that is directly relevant to high-density optical compute infrastructure where per-node authentication at line rate is required.

🇪🇺 EU Photonics & Manufacturing

EU Chips Act 2.0 Draft Formally Adds Integrated Photonics as a Strategic Technology

The European Commission's Chips Act 2.0 proposal formally adds integrated photonics as a key enabling semiconductor technology, broadening state-aid eligibility beyond front-end silicon fabs to cover specialty manufacturing, equipment, and materials — including InP, SiN, and SiPh platforms. The revision also drops the previous act's hard focus on leading-edge front-end fabs (sub-2nm CMOS), opening funding pathways for European photonics foundries for the first time under the Chips Act framework. Why it matters: A structural policy unlock for the European photonics industry — SMART Photonics, LioniX International, PhotonDelta ecosystem companies, and new entrants can now access Chips Act state-support mechanisms, changing the competitive dynamics for EU photonic compute manufacturing.
